Story

In my past life I was a Ph.D. student in the Humanities Department at York University. I led a weekly tutorial for six years, introducing students to traditional and popular culture, for a course, aptly, if not stoically, named, Introduction to Traditional and Popular Culture. We looked at ways in which groups of human beings — i.e. cultures — understood themselves and their surroundings through shared narrative practices. That is my more general term for this fundamental fact about human beings: they tell stories. Whereas today we might binge-watch some Netflix, historically a group might gather around the storyteller, then later buy a newspaper or attend the theatre, and still later gather around the radio in the living-room. You get the idea. But the point is, this ineluctable human impulse to make meaning doesn’t begin and end with storytelling as it might be popularly concieved.

As a matter of fact, I bet you have a certain idea in your head right now about what storytelling entails: it’s probably fictional, it’s probably designed primarily for entertainment, and you probably don’t take it very seriously.

But what if I told you that I used this (admittedly) cumbersome phrase, shared narrative practices because this idea extends far beyond storytelling as we might spontaneously understand it? Quantum theory is a shared narrative practice. Cognitive science? That’s a shared narrative practice, too. API documentation for iOS 10? You get the idea.
