Education

Summary

My background is in textual and cultural analysis and criticism, which I applied in my graduate studies to how language structures our experience and turns it into a meaningful exchange with others and the world around us. Broadly speaking, though, my education was an intensive study of critical thinking and problem solving. I break down ideas into their elements and consider how they operate together to create a cohesive, meaningful whole, a skill that I have since become profoundly interested in applying to UI/UX design and web development.

Additionally, I spent 8 years as a TA, using and developing these skills in a classroom environment in which we discussed how meaning operates at the level of various media, popular culture, and within the context of traditional (i.e. non-literate) cultures, before the advent of mass media.

Story

In my past life I was a Ph.D. student in the Humanities Department at York University. I led a weekly tutorial for six years, introducing students to traditional and popular culture, for a course, aptly, if not stoically, named, Introduction to Traditional and Popular Culture. We looked at ways in which groups of human beings — i.e. cultures — understood themselves and their surroundings through shared narrative practices. That is my more general term for this fundamental fact about human beings: they tell stories. Whereas today we might binge-watch some Netflix, historically a group might gather around the storyteller, then later buy a newspaper or attend the theatre, and still later gather around the radio in the living-room. You get the idea. But the point is, this ineluctable human impulse to make meaning doesn’t begin and end with storytelling as it might be popularly concieved.

As a matter of fact, I bet you have a certain idea in your head right now about what storytelling entails: it’s probably fictional, it’s probably designed primarily for entertainment, and you probably don’t take it very seriously.

But what if I told you that I used this (admittedly) cumbersome phrase, shared narrative practices because this idea extends far beyond storytelling as we might spontaneously understand it? Quantum theory is a shared narrative practice. Cognitive science? That’s a shared narrative practice, too. API documentation for iOS 10? You get the idea.

Research

Stasis Interrupted: Christianity and Subjectivity in David Fincher's Alien3

Published by Praeger in 2015 as part of a larger volume of articles examining christianity in popular culture, this article was the culmination of a lengthy research project examining representations of christianity in cinema. I chose to use Alien3 as a way of examining the meaning of Christianity in a modern context because of the way that the film merges religion (the 'born-again' colony of Prisoners that populate Fury 161) and redemption in a technologically advanced, post-industrial sci-fi universe. I was also interested in why the film -- despite being totally underrated -- stands as one of the most despised of the Alien series. The themes of Christianity and redemption in fact build upon and extend the previous two films in the series and, as a result, contribute excellently to a cohesive logic established with the first film. We move from diegetic universe in which belief in God has been fully evacuated due to the advancement of technology and science (the first Alien film) to one in which we are confronted with an attempt (by both Ripley and the colony of Prisoners) to reclaim faith in a world in which hope has been violently wrenched from it.
